About

Biography

Born in Dijon, France, flutist Isabelle Chapuis was a student of Jean-Pierre Rampal at the Conservatoire national supérieur de musique in Paris, where she was awarded the Premier Prix de flûte in 1970 and the Premier Prix de musique de chambre in 1971. In addition to Rampal, her teachers include many of the illustrious flutists in France, including Marcel Moyse, Michel Debost, Roger Boudin, Christian Lardé, Alain Marion, and Gaston Crunelle. From 1983 to 2018, she held the post of Principal Flute of Opera San José in California—in which she played in numerous performances in more than 110 operas. She also held at various times solo flute positions with the San Francisco Chamber Orchestra and the San Jose Chamber Orchestra.

On the faculty of the School of Music at San Jose State University for 33 years, she retired from university teaching in 2008 as Senior Lecturer in Flute, with an award from the President of San Jose State University for “outstanding professional attainment.” She continues to teach a limited number of advanced flute students at her home studio in Los Altos. In 2013, after her student Annie Wu was named a Presidential Scholar, Isabelle was cited by the U.S. Secretary of Education as one of the "most influential teachers" in the US; and she received a letter of recognition from President Obama. In her home town of Dijon, the Conservatoire de musique has named a flute competition: le Concours de flûte Isabelle Chapuis.

As a flute soloist, Isabelle has pursued an active career on both sides of the Atlantic. In Europe, she has performed as soloist with the Czech Chamber Orchestra, the Orchestre de chambre de Radio France (Paris), the Orchestre de chambre Jean-Francois Paillard, the Orchestre philharmonique de Dijon and L’Ensemble instrumental du Festival Estival de Paris. In the US, Isabelle has appeared as soloist with the San Francisco Chamber Orchestra, the Stockton Symphony, the Berkeley Symphony, the San Jose Chamber Orchestra, the Stanford Symphony, l’Orchestre de la Jeunesse Musicale in Brussels, the Greensboro (NC) Symphony, the San Jose Youth Symphony, the LaCrosse (Wisconsin) Symphony, and the San Jose State University Symphony.

The San Francisco Chronicle wrote: “Flutist Isabelle Chapuis was a sensation…performing with surety and technical control that recalled her famous teacher, Jean-Pierre Rampal.” Isabelle was the subject of a cover story on her career in Flute Talk Magazine. In 2022, the International Musician, (the magazine of the American Federation of Musicians) featured Isabelle in an article entitled “A Flutist’s Perfect Life.” Isabelle’s career is also featured in the new biography of her first teacher at the Paris Conservatory Gaston Crunelle, written by the American professor of music Leonard Garrison. Recently, she was interviewed on YouTube by flutist Robert Cart. Isabelle has served as a juror in the flute competition at the Conservatoire de musique in Marseille, France.

“French to her fingertips” (S. F. Chronicle), Isabelle has appeared in recital in the US and Europe on many prestigious series, including the Dame Myra Hess Series in Chicago (broadcast by NPR,) the Cleveland Museum of Art, the Milwaukee War Memorial, Radio France & France musique, the Legion of Honor in San Francisco, La Salle des Grands Ducs in Dijon, the Convention of the National Flute Association in St. Louis, and the Convention of the California Music Teachers Association in Santa Clara. Over a period of 20 years, Isabelle performed together with American flute virtuoso Robert Stallman and pianist Mark Anderson in recitals, chamber music concerts and master classes.

Education

Isabelle Chapuis studied at the Conservatoire National Supérieur de Musique de Paris, where she earned the Premier Prix de Flûte (1970) and the Premier Prix de Musique de Chambre (1971).

She studied with Maryse Gauci, Gaston Crunelle, Marcel Moyse, Michel Debost, Roger Bourdain, Christian Lardé, Alain Marion, and Jean-Pierre Rampal.

Teaching

Teaching is at the center of Isabelle’s work. She listens for the person behind the playing: the student’s sound, questions, courage, habits, imagination, and musical hopes.

Her teaching brings together discipline and joy. Lessons often return to the essentials: breath, tone, phrasing, rhythm, curiosity, and the search for a musical voice that feels honest and alive.

Isabelle works with advanced flute students privately and in masterclasses. Her aim is not to make students sound alike, but to help each player grow in confidence, clarity, and expressive freedom.

She takes pride in her students with affection and warmth, and many remain connected to her long after their formal studies have ended.
