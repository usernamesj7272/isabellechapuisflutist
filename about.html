Isabelle Chapuis was born in Dijon, France, where her love of the flute took root. Her first teacher was Maryse Gauci, whose guidance laid the foundation for her musical life. After only a year of study, Gauci introduced Isabelle to Gaston Crunelle. Isabelle was fourteen years old when he accepted her as his student. She later studied at the Conservatoire de Versailles, where Roger Bourdin was professor of flute, earning the Premier Prix de Flûte in her first year. She then entered the Conservatoire National Supérieur de Musique de Paris, where she continued her studies with Gaston Crunelle before studying with Jean-Pierre Rampal. There, she earned the Premier Prix de Flûte and the Premier Prix de Musique de Chambre. During those years, she also had the extraordinary privilege of learning from Marcel Moyse, Michel Debost, Christian Lardé, and Alain Marion. Their artistry, generosity, and devotion to music continue to influence her teaching and performing today.

In 1972, Isabelle married the American conductor Mark Starr, who had studied conducting with Franco Ferrara at the Conservatorio di Santa Cecilia in Rome. He later became assistant conductor to Igor Markevitch at the Orchestre National de Monte-Carlo and subsequently served as assistant to Jean Martinon in Belgium and the Netherlands.

The couple first moved to Milwaukee, where Mark was appointed Director of Orchestras at the University of Wisconsin–Milwaukee. They later settled in the San Francisco Bay Area when Mark became Director of Orchestras and Opera at Stanford University. California became the center of Isabelle’s musical life for the next five decades.

From 1983 to 2018, Isabelle served as Principal Flute of Opera San José, performing in more than 110 operatic productions over thirty-five seasons. At various times, she also held principal flute positions with the San Francisco Chamber Orchestra under the direction of Edgar Braun and the San Jose Chamber Orchestra.

As a soloist, Isabelle has performed on both sides of the Atlantic. In Europe, she has appeared with the Czech Chamber Orchestra, l’Orchestre de la Jeunesse Musicale in Brussels, the Orchestre de chambre de Radio France, the Orchestre de chambre Jean-François Paillard, the Orchestre philharmonique de Dijon, and L’Ensemble instrumental du Festival Estival de Paris. In the United States, she has appeared with the San Francisco Chamber Orchestra, the Stockton Symphony, the Berkeley Symphony, the San Jose Chamber Orchestra, the Stanford Symphony, the Greensboro Symphony, the La Crosse Symphony, the San Jose Youth Symphony, and the San José State University Symphony.

Recital performance has remained equally important throughout her career. She has appeared on series including the Dame Myra Hess Series in Chicago (broadcast by NPR), the Cleveland Museum of Art, the Milwaukee War Memorial, Radio France and France Musique, the Legion of Honor in San Francisco, La Salle des Grands Ducs in Dijon, the National Flute Association Convention, and the California Music Teachers Association Convention.

For almost twenty years, Isabelle had the joy of sharing the stage with her dear friend and colleague, the great flutist Robert Stallman. Over the course of their twenty-year friendship, they performed many duo recitals together, one of the great joys of her musical life. Robert left us much too early, and Isabelle remains deeply grateful for his constant inspiration and enduring friendship. One of Isabelle’s most treasured musical memories was Robert’s invitation to join him, harpist Kateřina Englichová, and the Czech Chamber Orchestra under the direction of Ondřej Kukal in recording Così fan Flauti: Mozart for Flute & Orchestra, including Mozart’s Concerto for Flute and Harp in C Major, K. 299.

Among the memorable experiences of her career was rehearsing Oiseaux exotiques with Olivier Messiaen and pianist Yvonne Loriod for the documentary Olivier Messiaen et les Oiseaux, an extraordinary experience with one of the twentieth century’s most visionary composers.

During her years at San José State University, Isabelle also enjoyed a longstanding musical partnership with pianist Mark Anderson, first as her class accompanist and later as a recital partner. Today, Anderson serves as Professor of Piano at the University of British Columbia, and Isabelle continues to treasure their long musical friendship.

Teaching has been one of the defining joys of Isabelle’s career. In 1975, she joined the School of Music faculty at San José State University, where she taught for thirty-three years before retiring in 2008 as Senior Lecturer in Flute. During her tenure, she received the University President’s Award for Outstanding Professional Attainment.

Throughout more than fifty years of teaching, Isabelle has watched generations of students grow into accomplished artists, educators, and remarkable human beings. Nothing has given her greater satisfaction than seeing them fulfill their dreams. Their successes have been among the greatest joys of her career. It has been especially meaningful to watch one of her former students, Ráyo Furuta, return to San José State University as Professor of Flute. Today, Isabelle continues to teach a limited number of advanced flute students from her home studio in Los Altos.

In 2013, after her student Annie Wu was named a Presidential Scholar, Isabelle was recognized by the United States Secretary of Education as one of the nation’s “most influential teachers” and received a letter of recognition from President Barack Obama. In her hometown of Dijon, the Conservatoire de musique established the Concours de flûte Isabelle Chapuis, a distinction that holds special meaning for her.

The San Francisco Chronicle once described Isabelle as “French to her fingertips” and wrote that she was “a sensation...performing with surety and technical control that recalled her famous teacher, Jean-Pierre Rampal.” She was the subject of a cover story in Flute Talk magazine, and in 2022 The International Musician, the magazine of the American Federation of Musicians, featured her career in an article titled A Flutist’s Perfect Life. Her work is also included in Leonard Garrison’s biography of Gaston Crunelle. She has been interviewed by flutist Robert Cart and was honored to be invited by the distinguished French piccoloist Jean-Louis Beaumadier to serve as a juror for the flute competition at the Conservatoire de musique in Marseille, France.

Music remains a central part of Isabelle’s family through her daughter, Jessica Starr, known professionally as Lulu, a violinist based in Hungary who is deeply immersed in traditional folk music and leads her own Budapest-based ensemble, Taraf de Akácfa. She has collaborated with distinguished folk musicians from many cultures around the world.

Looking back, Isabelle feels deeply grateful—for the teachers who believed in her and guided her, the colleagues who inspired her, the audiences who listened, and especially the students who entrusted her with a part of their musical journey. Music has given her a lifetime of friendships, discovery, and purpose, and she looks forward to continuing to share those gifts with future generations.
